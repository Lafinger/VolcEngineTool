# 反诈宣传页面_EN
Hello everyone! International students studying abroad alone often feel vulnerable. If you received a call from someone claiming to be from the police, procuratorate, or court, saying that you were suspected of a crime, would you panic? Recently, a branch of Postal Savings Bank in Zhengzhou, Henan Province, successfully intercepted an impersonation scam targeting an international student through sharp risk awareness, helping the customer avoid a huge loss of as much as 980,000 yuan. Today, let us review this real case.
On July 16, 2025, customer Ms. Liu called the bank to schedule a withdrawal of 980,000 yuan in cash for the following day. She sounded very nervous on the phone. Bank staff noticed that the amount was unusually large and that the account status was abnormal, so they decisively reported it to the Zhengzhou Anti-Fraud Center. The next day, when Ms. Liu arrived at the branch, she finally came to her senses after patient persuasion from the police and bank staff. It turned out that she had fallen into a carefully designed telecom fraud scheme.
How cunning were the scammers? Ms. Liu was an international student in Australia. In mid-June, she received a call from someone claiming to be from the local health department, saying that there was a record of her purchasing prohibited drugs. The call was then transferred to the so-called "Chinese police."
The scammers not only accurately stated Ms. Liu's personal information, but also falsely claimed that she was suspected of money laundering and faced 600 days of criminal detention. Then they changed their tone and said that as long as she paid a "security deposit" of 1.7 million yuan, she could avoid punishment. To gain her trust, the scammers even forged official documents and hypocritically claimed that they had already "advanced" 700,000 yuan on behalf of Ms. Liu because she was a student. Completely manipulated, Ms. Liu believed them without question. Under the scammers' remote instructions, she even returned to China specifically to withdraw the remaining 980,000 yuan in cash. Fortunately, the bank stopped her in time.
Here is the key point, everyone: international students have now become a high-risk target group for scams involving impersonation of police, procuratorate, and court authorities. The scammers' routine usually has two steps. First, they illegally obtain your name, passport number, school information, and other details, impersonate consulates or public security authorities, and create panic by claiming that you are suspected of transnational crimes. Second, once you become frightened, they transfer the call to fake domestic police officers, present forged official documents, and demand that you cooperate with their so-called investigation.
Here, we would like to solemnly remind all international students and parents: if you receive such a call, hang up immediately. Call 110 in China directly to verify the situation. Please remember: anyone who says your account is abnormal and must be frozen, anyone who asks you to transfer money to a "safe account," and anyone who asks you to pay a "security deposit" to solve the problem is a scammer.
Finally, we would also like to remind parents that while caring about their children's studies, they should also pay close attention to whether their children's financial needs appear unusual. Once you notice any warning signs, communicate in time and work together to guard against telecom and online fraud. Let us work together to protect the property safety of students studying overseas. Thank you for watching, and see you next time.
